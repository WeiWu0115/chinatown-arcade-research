# Axial Coding: Relationships Between Categories

## Methodology
Axial coding connects the open codes into higher-level categories and identifies relationships (causal conditions, context, strategies, consequences) using Strauss & Corbin's paradigm model.

---

## CATEGORY A: Embodied Interaction Quality

**Causal Conditions**: AR gesture-based photography as sole input; Snap Spectacles hardware constraints; outdoor public setting

**Properties & Dimensions**:
| Property | Dimension Range |
|----------|----------------|
| Gesture recognition reliability | Inconsistent ←→ Responsive |
| Feedback clarity | Absent ←→ Immediate |
| Physical comfort | Heavy/tight ←→ Light/natural |
| FOV adequacy | Too narrow ←→ Sufficient |

**Strategies participants used**:
- Repeated gestures until successful (P16, P07)
- Asked staff for help when stuck (P19)
- Adapted body position (bending, moving closer) to compensate for FOV (P04)

**Consequences**:
- When interaction works → flow state, delight, sense of agency ("it felt like a very cool move to make" — P04)
- When interaction fails → frustration, broken immersion, confusion about whether action registered

**Relationships**:
- **Interaction Quality → Narrative Immersion**: Broken interactions rupture the narrative flow; participants lose the "reporter" persona when struggling with gestures
- **Interaction Quality → Willingness to Pay**: "If it was a good UX... could easily charge $40-50" (P10) vs. lower WTP when UX has friction
- **Hardware Constraints → Interaction Quality**: FOV, battery, weight are upstream limiters that shape all downstream experience

## CATEGORY B: Spatial Navigation & Discovery

**Causal Conditions**: Scavenger hunt game mechanic; location-based AR; real urban environment with pedestrians and variable conditions

**Properties & Dimensions**:
| Property | Dimension Range |
|----------|----------------|
| Wayfinding clarity | Lost/confused ←→ Guided/clear |
| Discovery enjoyment | Frustrating search ←→ Rewarding find |
| Hint specificity | Too vague ←→ Too direct |
| Object accessibility | Too far/high ←→ Reachable/scannable |

**Core Tension**: **Guided vs. Self-Directed Exploration**
- Too much guidance → eliminates discovery joy, becomes "just following arrows"
- Too little guidance → participants get lost, confused, give up
- Sweet spot = progressive disclosure: broad hint → time-delayed specific hint → visual guide

**Strategies participants suggested**:
- Progressive hint system with time delays (P09, P10)
- Warmer/cooler feedback instead of direct pointing (P10)
- Sound cues for proximity (P11)
- 3D map with layered clue positions (P10)

**Relationships**:
- **Navigation → Duration**: Getting lost extends time but not in a fun way; better navigation could make same content feel richer
- **Navigation → Safety**: Wandering without awareness of surroundings creates street safety concerns (P01)
- **Navigation → Narrative**: Navigation IS the narrative — "finding clues" is the detective story; bad navigation breaks the story logic

## CATEGORY C: Narrative Depth & Meaning-Making

**Causal Conditions**: "City Theater" dramaturgical framework; 1900s Chinatown historical setting; role-play mechanic (reporter/police/chef/gangster)

**Properties & Dimensions**:
| Property | Dimension Range |
|----------|----------------|
| Story coherence | Fragmented tasks ←→ Connected narrative arc |
| Historical depth | Surface-level objects ←→ Immersive period experience |
| Role embodiment | Just wearing a label ←→ Acting in character |
| Cultural education | Incidental learning ←→ Deep understanding |

**Core Tension**: **Entertainment vs. Education**
- "Who comes to study? Who? I'll call everyone over to play, and I'll let you study" (P19)
- Fun must lead; education follows as a byproduct
- "Learning can be taught to us... you play, we come to study" (P19)
- Most participants want to FEEL the history, not READ about it

**Sub-categories**:
1. **Historical Visualization**: Strong desire to see the past overlaid on present ("overlay historical view... 100 years ago" — P20; "archival photos" — P22; "tenement museum" feeling — P13)
2. **Story Continuity**: Current experience feels like discrete tasks, not a flowing narrative (P03, P19)
3. **Multiplayer Narrative**: Different roles should intertwine (P07, P03)

**Relationships**:
- **Narrative → Emotional Engagement**: When story connects → participants express deeper emotional responses about culture and history
- **Narrative → WTP**: Richer story justifies higher price; current basic narrative limits perceived value
- **Narrative → Repeat Visits**: Multiple role tracks = multiple visits ("charge 20, I come back for the other track" — P10)

## CATEGORY D: Social-Material Ecology

**Causal Conditions**: Public street setting; group play; physical artifacts (postcard, lanyard); local businesses present

**Properties & Dimensions**:
| Property | Dimension Range |
|----------|----------------|
| Social configuration | Solo ←→ Group/multiplayer |
| Spectator effect | Embarrassing ←→ Cool/attention-positive |
| Physical-digital integration | Disconnected ←→ Seamless blend |
| Local business integration | None ←→ Deep partnership |

**Sub-categories**:
1. **Spectacle of Wearing AR**: Being watched creates mixed feelings — some love it ("on the leading edge" — P10), some find it "awkward" (P03)
2. **Physical Artifacts as Bridge**: Postcard serves as physical anchor, but also creates ergonomic issues (holding card + gesturing)
3. **Local Commerce Loop**: Food, discounts, vendor partnerships create reason to engage beyond the AR experience

**Strategies suggested by participants**:
- Staff uniforms to signal "event happening" (P19)
- Restaurant partnerships for post-experience dining (P04, P10, P13)
- Souvenirs that connect to local businesses (VIP card, coupons)
- Postcard as 3D AR artifact (Web AR extension) (P10)

**Relationships**:
- **Social Ecology → Monetization**: Partnerships with local businesses = revenue diversification beyond ticket sales
- **Social Ecology → Cultural Value**: Connecting to real places & people deepens heritage meaning
- **Physical Artifacts → Extended Experience**: Souvenirs extend the experience beyond the 10-minute play session

## CATEGORY E: Commercial Viability & Scaling

**Causal Conditions**: Novel technology; no market precedent in NYC; development hardware; small team

**Properties & Dimensions**:
| Property | Dimension Range |
|----------|----------------|
| Pricing confidence | "No idea" ←→ Strong opinion |
| Target market clarity | Vague ←→ Specific segment |
| Revenue model | Ticket-only ←→ Multi-stream |
| Scalability | Single location ←→ Multi-city |

**Key Insight from P15 (ex-Meta)**: "Monetization will come with partnerships from either the city or tourism... not straight to user" — B2B before B2C

**Revenue Model Options Emerged**:
1. **Direct ticket**: $20-30 baseline for current 10-min experience
2. **Tour integration**: Combined with walking tour → $50-100+
3. **Business partnerships**: Featured in restaurant/store → sponsorship revenue
4. **Education**: School programs → $200-500 per student (P15)
5. **Merchandise**: Blind boxes, plushies, postcards, keychains
6. **Multiple tracks**: Repeat visits for different roles

**Relationships**:
- **UX Quality → Pricing Power**: Better UX = more justified higher price
- **Content Depth → Duration → Price**: More content = longer experience = higher price tolerance
- **Partnerships → Sustainability**: Single revenue stream is fragile; multiple streams create resilience

## CATEGORY F: Technology as Cultural Bridge

**Causal Conditions**: Chinatown as historically rich but underexplored district; AR as medium for "making invisible visible"

**Properties & Dimensions**:
| Property | Dimension Range |
|----------|----------------|
| Cultural accessibility | Insider-only knowledge ←→ Open to all |
| Heritage visibility | Hidden/forgotten ←→ Activated/perceptible |
| Community impact | Tourist extraction ←→ Community enrichment |
| Generational relevance | Only for tech-savvy ←→ Cross-generational |

**Core Insight**: AR doesn't just display information — it transforms the ACT OF LOOKING into the act of cultural discovery. Photography-as-interaction makes participants active investigators rather than passive tourists.

**Relationships**:
- **Cultural Bridge → Social Validation**: Non-Chinese participants and industry experts validate the cultural value, not just the tech
- **Cultural Bridge → Monetization**: Cultural significance provides moral/social justification for public funding, tourism board partnerships
- **Cultural Bridge → Design Philosophy**: Prioritizes connection over isolation; outdoor over indoor; embodied over screen-based
