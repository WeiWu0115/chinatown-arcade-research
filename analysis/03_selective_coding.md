# Selective Coding: Core Category & Emerging Theory

## Core Category: "Embodied Cultural Discovery Through Augmented Perception"

The central phenomenon that emerges across all interviews is that **AR technology, when deployed in a culturally rich urban environment, transforms passive spectatorship into embodied cultural investigation** — but this transformation is fragile, mediated by interaction quality, narrative coherence, and social-material conditions.

## The Theory: "Augmented Heritage Theater"

### Proposition 1: Photography-as-Interaction Creates Embodied Agency
The design choice of hand-gesture photography as the sole interaction mechanism transforms participants from viewers into investigators. When this mechanism works reliably, it creates a powerful sense of agency — "it felt like a very cool move to make" (P04), "it's like embodied visualization" (P20). When it fails, it is the single most disruptive factor to the entire experience.

**Evidence**: Every single participant commented on the gesture interaction. It is the fulcrum of the experience.

### Proposition 2: The Navigation Paradox — Guided Enough to Progress, Lost Enough to Discover
There exists an optimal "discovery tension" between being guided and being lost. Participants simultaneously want:
- To know roughly where to go (avoid frustration)
- To NOT know exactly what they'll find (preserve surprise)

The current experience errs toward too little guidance. The ideal system uses **progressive disclosure**: broad hint → time delay → specific hint → visual guide. This mirrors how real investigation works — you start with a lead and narrow down.

**Evidence**: P09 ("start with a broad hint"), P10 ("phone a friend" / time-based hints), P11 ("if he doesn't find it after so many times, add a second hint"), P19 ("the continuity needs to be improved")

### Proposition 3: Narrative Is the Multiplier, Not the Feature
The AR technology and scavenger mechanics are necessary but insufficient. What transforms the experience from "tech demo" to "cultural product" is narrative depth. Current experience = Collection of tasks. Desired experience = Living story.

Three narrative dimensions identified:
1. **Temporal layering**: Seeing the past overlaid on the present (most requested feature)
2. **Character embodiment**: Actually living the role, not just labeled as one
3. **Plot interconnection**: Actions building toward a climax and resolution

**Evidence**: P03 ("nail the narrative... that's going to be a really powerful thing"), P13 ("feel what it was like 100 years ago"), P22 ("archival visual elements"), P20 ("all the stops form a continuous story"), P07 ("stories intertwine")

### Proposition 4: The Experience Extends Beyond the Glasses
The AR experience is not bounded by the 10-minute play session. It extends into:
- **Before**: Promotional videos set expectations (P19 notes video vs. reality gap)
- **During**: Physical artifacts (postcard, lanyard, map) anchor the experience
- **After**: Souvenirs, food, local business visits, social sharing

The most commercially viable and culturally meaningful version integrates ALL of these phases. A postcard that becomes a 3D AR map at home. A restaurant visit that's part of the narrative. A souvenir that's also a discount card.

**Evidence**: P10 (3D map postcard idea, VIP vendor card), P13 (wanting real dumplings not plush), P04 (business partnership for foot traffic), P15 (tour integration model)

### Proposition 5: AR Tourism Legitimacy Requires Social Proof Beyond Tech
The experience gains legitimacy not just from technology, but from:
- **Cultural endorsement**: Chinatown community figures validating it (P14, P21)
- **Industry validation**: XR experts confirming viability (P03, P04, P15, P20)
- **Investor interest**: Financial sector paying attention (P17)
- **Global context**: Not just "cool tech" but positioned within worldwide AR tourism trend (P12)

This multi-stakeholder validation is essential for the B2B partnerships that P15 identifies as the real monetization path.

## Emerging Grounded Theory Statement

> **"City Theater" as an AR heritage experience succeeds to the degree that it transforms the urban streetscape into a stage where participants become active investigators of cultural history through embodied gesture, spatial navigation, and narrative immersion. The quality of this transformation depends on a chain of mediating factors — interaction reliability, progressive wayfinding, narrative coherence, and social-material support — each of which can either amplify or break the illusion. Commercial sustainability emerges not from the technology itself, but from the experience's capacity to serve as a "cultural bridge" that connects tourists to local businesses, history to present, and digital spectacle to physical place.**

## Actionable Design Implications

Based on this grounded theory analysis:

| Priority | Implication | Evidence Strength |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Fix gesture feedback loop** — immediate visual/audio confirmation when photo captures | Very strong (nearly universal) |
| 2 | **Implement progressive hint system** — broad → specific → visual, time-based | Strong (multiple independent suggestions) |
| 3 | **Deepen narrative continuity** — connect acts into flowing story, not discrete tasks | Strong |
| 4 | **Create onboarding flow** — 2-min in-lens tutorial before game starts | Strong |
| 5 | **Develop local business partnerships** — post-experience dining/shopping | Moderate-strong |
| 6 | **Historical overlay (future)** — 3D Gaussian Splatting of period streetscapes | Strong desire, technical dependency |
| 7 | **Multi-role multiplayer** — intertwining storylines for group play | Moderate |
| 8 | **Souvenir ecosystem** — AR-enabled postcard + vendor VIP card + blind box | Moderate |

## Limitations & Reflexivity

1. **Participant bias**: Many participants are XR professionals or tech-adjacent — their tolerance for hardware limitations is higher than general public
2. **Social desirability**: Interviews conducted immediately post-experience by the team → politeness bias
3. **Single-site**: All data from Manhattan Chinatown Doyers Street area
4. **Translation artifacts**: Some DJI transcripts (especially P19/Kefan) were auto-translated from Chinese, potentially losing nuance
5. **Group dynamics**: Group interviews (P10, P11-P13) may reflect conformity rather than independent opinions
6. **LLM-assisted coding**: This analysis was performed with LLM assistance; a human researcher should validate codes against raw transcripts before publication
